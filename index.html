Overall Introduction

The video game industry has evolved from a niche hobby into a global economic powerhouse. In 2024, the global video game market generated $184.3 billion in revenue ( app2top.com ) while U.S. consumer spending alone reached $58.7 billion, marking a 106% increase over the past decade from $28.4 billion in 2014 ( theesa.com ). These figures tell the story of an industry that has grown beyond to become a cultural force, now standing shoulder-to-shoulder with established entertainment mediums such as film and music.

The gaming industry is also booming because of its diversity. China and the United States together account for over half of global gaming revenue, generating $48.7 billion and $47.6 billion, respectively, yet markets like Japan and South Korea are also not far behind (visualcapitalist). This industry spans multiple platforms—consoles, PCs, and mobile devices—each with different player behaviors, preferences, and spending patterns.

The video game market spans platforms and genres, appealing to players of all different backgrounds and interests. As a major mode of recreation and storytelling, we’re interested in understanding what drives success in this dynamic market. With rapidly evolving trends in genres, platforms, and monetization strategies, analyzing historical sales data can reveal valuable patterns about what resonates with audiences across different regions and demographics. This dataset allows us to explore which games, publishers, and platforms have shaped the industry's remarkable trajectory. Through visualizations, we explore trends over time to interpret what platforms and publishers are popular over the years. We compare genres, developers, and platforms to consider what types of games are successful with a focus on differences in regions.

About the Data

This data was downloaded from Kaggle and contains a list of video games with a sales count of greater than 100,000 copies. The user who posted the dataset created it by scraping vgchartz.com, a video game sales tracking website based in the UK. The data contains a column attribute called “rank,” corresponding to how the video games are ordered on the site by number of sales. It also contains the columns Name, Year, Platform, Genre, Publisher, North American Sales, European Sales, Japan Sales, sales for everywhere except those three regions, and Global Sales, which is total worldwide sales. Values in each sales column represent sales in millions. The dataset includes video games published between 1980 and 2020, inclusive.

The original dataset contained 16,598 rows. To reduce the size to better fit the guidelines of this project, we preprocessed the data by dropping the rows containing null values and filtered the data to contain only video games published between 2000 and 2015, inclusive. This reduces our data set to just under 14,000 rows.

Comparing Across Regions

The structure of the data lends itself to comparison of sales in different regions, and that's where we started in order to investigate what might make a game successful, and if that's different depending on the targeted region.

Each of these bar charts shows the top ten publishers by the total number of sales across all of their games. We can see that while the top publishers based on sales in North America and Europe are very similar, the ranking of Publishers by Japansese sales is notably different. Although Nintendo is a top two publisher across all regions, in Japan it has displaced Electronic Arts for first place, and has a significantly higher number of sales than even the second place publisher.

It seems there may be a significant difference in sales trends between Japan and North America/Europe, but there are a variety of reasons a certain publisher might be more popular than others. Beyond the quality of the games they produce, they may specialize in a certain popular genre or publish games for a popular platform.

These bar charts depict sales in millions by video game genre. The top bar chart is a side-by-side bar chart with the different colored bars representing different market regions: Europe, Japan, North America, and all other regions. You can select which regions you want dispalyed in this chart by clicking the corresponding colored square in the legend. To make more than one selection when clicking on the legend, hold down the shift key. The chart below is video game sales by genre, globally. The genres on both charts are sorted in descending order by global sales. By default, the charts show data for the games released between 2000 and 2015, or you can select which year you want to view specifically from the drop down.

From this visualization, we can see that North America seems to dominate sales, being the region with the most sales for every genre. North America's lead in total sales was visibile in the previous bar graphs, but is clearer here when placed side-by-side with other regions instead of relying on reading and remembering the numerical sales counts.

Genres have a similar ranking by North American and European sales, which follows the same trend as Global Sales. By ordering the genres in the upper chart in same way as the bottom chart, in decending order by Global Sales, it is made clear which regions follow and inform the global trend, and which diverge, especially when toggling different regions on and off.

For most genres, North American sales are significantly higher than every other region, with the exception of Role-Playing, where Japanese sales are very close in count, indicating that Japanase sales in this genre a playing an important role in its global ranking by sales.

Comparing Across Platforms

From this treemap, it is evident that Nintendo and Electronic Arts have consistently dominated the gaming market over the years, recording total global sales of over 2,360M and about 1,992M, respectively. Nintendo’s strongest contribution comes from the Wii, which accounts for around 780M in global sales, while Electronic Arts’ leading platform is the PS2, contributing about 491M units.

The hierarchical nature of this treemap effectively demonstrates how publishers' output is comprised, and highights similarities and differences across publishers. For example, some publishers are focused on producing content for their own platforms, like Nintendo and Microsoft. At the same time, the Wii and PS2 are consistently in the top platforms across publishers. This is likely because these were recent consoles from major developers during the time range we're looking at (2000-2015), but prompts interesting questions for further outside research about what exactly makes a gaming console successful.

From this Sankey diagram, using a sales threshold of 141M, the Action genre emerges as the dominant category, spanning three major platforms — X360, PS3, and PS2 — with cumulative sales exceeding 800 M. The Shooter genre follows as the second-highest contributor, concentrated primarily on X360 and PS3, with combined sales of 472.9M. In contrast, the Racing genre appears more limited in scope, being associated only with the PS2 at this threshold and having lower sales. As the sales threshold increases, Shooter, Sports, and Action remain the most prominent genres, each maintaining total sales less than 286M but larger than 271M with the majority of their revenue originating from the North American market.

When the threshold is reduced to 46 million units, additional genres — including Simulation, Puzzle, Role-Playing, Fighting, and Adventure — become visible. These genres demonstrate lower overall sales and are distributed primarily across less-popular or niche platforms such as the DS, 3DS, GBA, PSP, Xbox, and Xbox One, while also appearing on more widely adopted consoles like the Xbox 360 and Wii. This pattern suggests that while these genres attract smaller audiences, they maintain broader platform diversity.

This diagram allows for the exploration of the connection between genre and platform considered earlier, demonstrating what type of game is driving the popularity of different platforms, like sports games for the Wii. The sales threshold slider allows for clear overview of how specific genres and platforms dominate at a high level while still allowing for the exploration of less popular ones.

Summary and Future Work

Through these data visualizations it’s clear that video game popularity is shaped by an interplay of genre preference, regional differences, platform availability, and publisher influence. Blockbuster genres dominate major consoles, niche genres spread across diverse platforms, and publisher dominance shifts over time and varies by region. As new players enter the market and hardware generations change, what makes a game popular will continue to fluctuate.

Unfortunately, one of the limits of this dataset is that it’s out of date, focusing on games released between 2000 and 2015. For this reason, although we are able to engage with differences in categories like market region and genre, publishers and especially platforms in this dataset are not current.

In the future, the scope of this project could be expanded by examining current data that reflects new consoles like the PS5 or the Nintendo Switch, as well as tying in data about console sales to better understand how the success of a platform influences the popularity of a game.
